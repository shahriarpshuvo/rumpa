## What BBT Actually Tells You ← targets: "BBT charting"
- BBT is your lowest resting body temperature, taken first thing in the morning before getting up.
- After ovulation, progesterone causes a sustained rise of roughly 0.5 to 1°F (0.3 to 0.6°C).
- The pattern (low pre-ovulation, sustained higher post-ovulation) is what matters, not any single reading.
- `<Cite n="1" />` StatPearls physiology reference.

## The Accuracy Problem (Be Honest About It) ← targets: long-tail "does BBT confirm ovulation reliably"
- Prospective studies show that BBT readings only coincide with actual ovulation day in roughly 22% of cycles when compared with ultrasound or LH-confirmed timing.
- The temperature shift happens 1 to 2 days *after* ovulation, sometimes longer. By the time you see the rise, the fertile window has typically closed.
- `<Cite n="2" />` Comparative diagnostic accuracy study (Wrist temperature vs BBT).
- `<Callout type="clinical-tip">` "Patients who come to me hoping BBT will predict their fertile window, I gently redirect. BBT is a confirmation tool, not a prediction tool."

## What Disrupts BBT Readings ← targets: long-tail "best time to take BBT each morning"
- Less than 3 to 4 consecutive hours of sleep before measurement.
- Inconsistent wake time (more than ~30 minutes drift).
- Alcohol the night before, fever or illness, shift work or jet lag, electric blankets, room temperature swings, restless sleep with a partner or pet.
- Practical guide: same time every morning, before standing, talking, or drinking water; thermometer kept within arm's reach.

## BBT vs OPK, and When to Use Which ← targets: "BBT vs OPK"
- OPKs (ovulation predictor kits) detect the LH surge that precedes ovulation by 24 to 36 hours; they predict the fertile window.
- BBT confirms that ovulation happened after the fact; it cannot time intercourse for *this* cycle.
- For most TTC patients, OPKs (or a fertility monitor) are the better front-line tool. BBT is best used alongside OPKs to confirm the surge translated into actual ovulation.

## When BBT Is Genuinely Useful ← bridges to escalation and luteal-phase concerns
- Confirming that ovulation is happening (a flat chart over several cycles is information).
- Estimating luteal phase length (a short luteal phase, under 10 days, is worth raising with your doctor).
- Providing chart data when you escalate care, your GP, OB-GYN, or fertility specialist can see at a glance whether you are ovulating and when.
- `<Callout type="note">` "If BBT charting is adding stress without adding information, it is reasonable to stop. TTC is hard enough without a 6am alarm you dread."

## What This Means for You (NOT titled "Conclusion")
- 2-3 sentences. The honest answer on basal body temperature TTC accuracy is that BBT confirms ovulation reliably for the previous cycle, but it does not predict the fertile window for the current one. Use it alongside OPKs or as a check that your cycle is ovulatory, not as your only timing tool.
