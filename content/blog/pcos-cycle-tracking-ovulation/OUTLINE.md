## Hook (2-3 sentences)
- Cycle tracking that works for a 28-day cycle often falls apart with PCOS. PCOS cycle tracking ovulation requires layered tools, because chronically elevated LH, irregular follicle development, and absent temperature shifts can confuse the standard methods. This post sets out what works, what does not, and when to stop tracking and start treating.

## Why standard tracking fails in PCOS ← targets: "irregular cycle ovulation detection"
- Chronically high baseline LH (around 70 to 80 percent of PCOS cases) means OPKs misread
- Multiple LH surges in one cycle, often without an actual egg release
- Follicle waves that build, stall, and reset, so the fertile window can shift week to week
- BBT can be flat because ovulation never happens that cycle
- `<Callout type="note">` This is biology, not user error, do not blame your tracking technique for irregular signals
- `<Cite n="1" />` 2023 International Evidence-based PCOS Guideline

## Do ovulation tests work if you have PCOS ← targets: "OPK with PCOS"
- Standard LH OPKs: useful only if your baseline LH is normal, many PCOS cases get false positives
- Long-tail answered in prose: "do ovulation tests work if you have PCOS" yes, sometimes, with caveats
- Strategy: test from day 10 in a 28-day cycle, from day 14 to 21 in a 35 to 45-day cycle, stop after a sustained positive plus confirmation
- PdG (pregnanediol glucuronide) urine strips after a suspected surge to confirm ovulation actually happened
- Quantitative LH and oestrogen monitors (Mira, Inito, Clearblue Advanced) can read changing baselines, more useful in PCOS than basic strips
- `<Cite n="2" />` PCOS Guideline on ovulation monitoring

## BBT for PCOS, what your chart will actually show ← targets: "BBT for PCOS"
- Confirmatory tool, not a predictor, a sustained rise of 0.2 to 0.5 degrees Celsius for 3 days suggests ovulation has happened
- In anovulatory cycles the chart stays flat or rises late, useful diagnostic information for your doctor
- Take temperature at the same time each morning before getting up, log with an app
- Bring 2 to 3 months of charts to your appointment, they shorten the conversation
- `<Callout type="clinical-tip">` A flat chart is data, not failure, it is evidence that ovulation needs medical help that cycle

## Cervical mucus and cycle day, the underused signals
- Egg-white mucus around the fertile window even with PCOS
- Cycle length tracking over 3 months identifies whether you ovulate at all and how often
- Stack signals: mucus plus OPK plus BBT plus PdG gives the clearest read, no single test does

## How to predict ovulation with irregular cycles ← targets: "how to predict ovulation with irregular cycles"
- Pattern of 35 to 60 day cycles: extend testing window, start day 12, continue to day 30
- Pattern of less than 8 periods a year (oligo or anovulatory): tracking alone will not solve it, ovulation induction is the next conversation
- Quantitative hormone monitors gain a real advantage here
- Bridge to Rumpa post on letrozole vs clomid for PCOS

## When to stop tracking and ask for ovulation induction
- 6 cycles of confirmed-anovulation, or fewer than 8 periods a year and TTC, talk to your OB-GYN about letrozole or clomiphene
- NICE and the 2023 PCOS Guideline both name letrozole as first-line ovulation induction in PCOS
- Pre-treatment workup: baseline bloods, ultrasound to check uterine and ovarian anatomy, partner semen analysis
- `<Cite n="3" />` NICE NG257 on ovulation induction
- `<Cite n="4" />` 2023 PCOS Guideline first-line letrozole recommendation

## What This Means for You
- PCOS cycle tracking ovulation works best when you stack tools, OPK plus BBT plus cervical mucus, and add a quantitative monitor or PdG if your cycles are irregular. If 2 to 3 cycles of careful tracking show no clear ovulation, that is the signal to escalate, not to track harder.
