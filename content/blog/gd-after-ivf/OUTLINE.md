## Hook (2-3 sentences)
- Many patients hear about gestational diabetes risk after IVF for the first time at their booking appointment and feel blindsided. The risk is real but often smaller than headlines suggest, and a lot of it is explained by the same reasons you needed IVF in the first place. Here's how I talk it through in clinic.

## Does IVF actually raise gestational diabetes risk? ← targets: "IVF pregnancy complications"
- "Does IVF increase gestational diabetes risk?" answered in prose
- Honest summary of the evidence: small but real signal above background, magnitude depends on confounders
- Confounders: maternal age, BMI, PCOS, infertility duration
- `<Callout type="clinical-tip">` IVF is rarely the only variable, it travels with other risk factors
- `<Cite n="1" />` JCEM 2022 blastocyst study, ADA 2026 standards

## Fresh vs frozen embryo transfer, what the data shows ← targets: "frozen embryo transfer outcomes"
- 2020 meta-analysis: fresh transfer associated with higher GD risk vs natural conception, frozen transfer roughly half the odds of fresh
- Why fresh vs frozen physiology differs (corpus luteum, oestrogen levels, endometrial environment)
- Newer signal: longer embryo culture and blastocyst transfer may add risk inside the FET group
- Plain English: this matters for the conversation, not for second-guessing a transfer you have already done

## Hidden risk multipliers most IVF pregnancies carry
- PCOS, advanced maternal age, BMI above 30, family history of type 2 diabetes
- Multiple pregnancy adds risk independently
- Bridge to internal Rumpa post on PCOS and gestational diabetes link

## Early screening, what to ask for and when ← targets: "early GD screening for IVF pregnancy"
- "Should IVF pregnancies get early GD testing?" answered in prose
- Standard OGTT at 24-28 weeks (NHS, NICE)
- Many specialists add an early screen at 12-16 weeks for high-risk groups (BMI, PCOS, prior GD, family history)
- ACOG and NICE both support early screening in high-risk patients
- `<Callout type="note">` Ask your team explicitly, early screening is not automatic everywhere

## What you can change between transfer and screening
- Modest weight stability, not weight loss
- Mediterranean-pattern eating, daily movement, sleep
- Continuous glucose monitor use is increasingly common in IVF pregnancies with risk factors
- `<Callout type="warning">` This is about lowering risk, not preventing GD. If you develop it, that's biology, not a personal failure

## What This Means for You
- Gestational diabetes risk after IVF is slightly higher than the background rate, mostly because of the same factors that led to IVF. Ask for early screening if you have BMI above 30, PCOS, or a family history of diabetes, and use the time between transfer and 16 weeks to set up sustainable habits.
