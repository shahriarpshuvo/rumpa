## The Short Answer (Lead With Clarity) ← targets: "PCOS renaming PMOS"
- PMOS stands for Polyendocrine Metabolic Ovarian Syndrome. It is the same condition you knew as PCOS, with a more accurate name.
- The 2026 international consensus, published in The Lancet and announced by the Endocrine Society, did not redraw the disease. It renamed it.
- `<Callout type="note">` "Bold takeaway: if you have a PCOS diagnosis today, you have a PMOS diagnosis tomorrow. Same condition, more accurate label."
- `<Cite n="1" />` Endocrine Society 2026 announcement.

## Why the Name Changed ← targets: "polycystic metabolic ovarian syndrome"
- The old name implied pathological ovarian cysts. What ultrasound shows is arrested follicles, not cysts.
- The condition involves insulin resistance, androgen excess, and neuroendocrine signalling. It is metabolic and endocrine, not just ovarian.
- Diagnostic delay affected up to 70% of patients under the old framing.
- The renaming process took roughly a decade and involved around 22,000 experts and over 14,000 patient and clinician survey responses.
- `<Cite n="2" />` The Lancet / STAT coverage.

## What Stays the Same in Diagnosis ← targets: "PCOS diagnostic criteria update"
- The Rotterdam criteria, upgraded by the 2023 International Evidence-Based Guideline, still apply. Diagnosis in adults still requires two of three: clinical or biochemical hyperandrogenism, ovulatory dysfunction, polycystic ovaries on ultrasound (or elevated AMH as a 2023 alternative).
- If you already have a diagnosis, you do not need a new ultrasound, a new blood test, or a new GP appointment to "convert" it.
- `<Cite n="3" />` ASRM 2023 guideline summary.
- `<Callout type="clinical-tip">` "Patients who come to me worried that they need to redo every test, I reassure them: the criteria didn't change, only the name did."

## Does PMOS Change Treatment or TTC Plans? ← targets: long-tail "does PMOS change PCOS treatment"
- For TTC: ovulation-induction options (letrozole as first line, clomiphene as alternative, inositol and metformin as adjuncts where indicated) are unchanged by the renaming.
- For metabolic care: the new name actually reinforces what good clinicians were already doing, screening for insulin resistance, lipid profile, blood pressure, and type 2 diabetes risk.
- For pregnancy: PCOS / PMOS is still a known risk factor for gestational diabetes (bridge to `pcos-and-gd-link`).
- The treatment guidelines, the prescriptions, and the monitoring you may already be on are untouched.

## Long-Tail: Is PMOS Replacing PCOS Officially Everywhere? ← targets: long-tail "is PMOS replacing PCOS officially"
- Yes, by international consensus published in 2026. Adoption into electronic health records, insurance codes, NHS clinical letters, and patient-facing materials will roll out over the next 12 to 24 months.
- Expect both names to coexist for some time. Your medical records may say PCOS, PMOS, or both. Either is correct.
- If you live in the UK, your GP letter may still read PCOS for now. That is not an error.
