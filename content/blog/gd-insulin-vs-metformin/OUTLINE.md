## Hook (2-3 sentences)
- When patients hear "we need to start medication", the first question is almost always about gestational diabetes insulin vs metformin and which one is safer. The honest answer is that both are used, the choice depends partly on your numbers and partly on where in the world you live. Here's what each medication does, and what to expect from your team.

## How the GD medication decision is made ← targets: "first-line GD medication"
- Diet and movement is the starting point for everyone
- Medication is added if fasting or post-meal targets are missed roughly 30% of the time over one to two weeks
- "Will I need insulin for gestational diabetes?" answered in prose
- `<Callout type="clinical-tip">` Needing medication is biology, not behaviour
- `<Cite n="1" />` NICE NG3, ACOG, ADA Standards of Care 2026

## Metformin in pregnancy, what it does and what the data shows ← targets: "metformin pregnancy safety"
- Mechanism: improves insulin sensitivity, lowers liver glucose output, tablet form
- "Is metformin safe throughout pregnancy?" answered in prose: large datasets up to school age look reassuring, longer-term data still emerging
- Practical notes: GI side effects, taken with meals, gradual dose titration
- Crosses the placenta (a known fact, included transparently)
- About half of GD pregnancies started on metformin will need insulin added at some point

## Insulin in pregnancy, the gold standard explained ← targets: "insulin injection in pregnancy"
- Does not cross the placenta, longest safety record of any GD medication
- Rapid-acting before meals (NovoRapid, Humalog), sometimes intermediate-acting at night (Insulatard, Humulin I)
- Pen device, fine needle, most patients learn it in one clinic session
- Doses rise through pregnancy as placental hormones rise, and that is expected, not failure

## UK vs US, why guidelines disagree on first-line
- NICE (UK) since 2020: metformin first if diet fails, insulin added or used if metformin contraindicated or declined
- ACOG and ADA (US): insulin preferred first-line, metformin an option if insulin declined or unavailable
- SMFM (US): metformin acceptable first-line, equivalent
- `<Callout type="note">` This is one of the clearest examples of identical data, different professional society positions

## What changes day-to-day depending on the choice
- Logistics: tablets vs pen injections, fridge storage for insulin, supply on holiday
- Monitoring: same finger-prick schedule, possibly continuous glucose monitor in either case
- Birth plan implications: most centres treat metformin-only and insulin-treated GD similarly for delivery timing, with individual variation
- Bridge to internal Rumpa post on bedtime snacks if fasting is the main driver

## What This Means for You
- Gestational diabetes insulin vs metformin is a real choice, not a hierarchy. NICE and ACOG genuinely disagree, both medications have a long track record, and the practical question is which one fits your numbers, your tolerance, and your clinic's pathway. If your team has recommended one and you want a second opinion before starting, that's a fair conversation to have. `<ConsultCTA placement="end" />`
