## What PCOS Looks Like Day to Day ← targets: "PCOS symptoms checklist"
- Plain-English symptoms: irregular or absent periods, acne after adolescence, hirsutism (chin/upper lip/abdomen hair), scalp thinning, weight gain around the middle, difficulty conceiving.
- Long-tail answered in prose: "what are the early signs of PCOS".

## The Rotterdam Criteria, Explained ← targets: "Rotterdam criteria PCOS"
- 2 of 3 features: irregular ovulation, clinical or biochemical hyperandrogenism, polycystic ovarian morphology on ultrasound.
- Why "polycystic ovaries on a scan" alone is not PCOS.
- `<Cite n="1" />` 2023 International Evidence-Based PCOS Guideline.

## What Blood Tests Confirm PCOS? ← targets: "PCOS diagnosis blood tests"
- Standard panel: total/free testosterone, SHBG, free androgen index, LH/FSH, TSH, prolactin, 17-hydroxyprogesterone, fasting glucose + HbA1c, lipid profile.
- Cycle timing for testing (day 2-5 if cycling, any day if not).
- `<Callout type="clinical-tip">` Anti-Müllerian hormone (AMH, an egg-reserve blood marker) is often raised in PCOS but is not on its own diagnostic.

## Conditions That Mimic PCOS (and Must Be Ruled Out)
- Thyroid dysfunction, hyperprolactinaemia, non-classical congenital adrenal hyperplasia, Cushing's, androgen-secreting tumours.
- This is why a checklist alone cannot diagnose, and why a clinician matters.
- Long-tail answered in prose: "which doctor diagnoses PCOS" (GP first in UK, OB-GYN/endocrinologist in US, REI if fertility is the driver).

## NHS vs US Pathway to a PCOS Diagnosis
- **UK / NHS:** GP appointment, baseline bloods, pelvic ultrasound referral if indicated, gynaecology or endocrinology referral for complex cases.
- **US:** OB-GYN or primary care first, direct referral to endocrinology or REI depending on insurance and the dominant concern.
