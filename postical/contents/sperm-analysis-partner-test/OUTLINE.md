## When Sperm Analysis Is Usually Recommended ← targets: "semen analysis explained"
- Standard guideline: 12 months of regular unprotected sex without pregnancy if the woman is under 35; 6 months if she's 35 or older.
- NICE CG156 / NG257 (UK) and ASRM (US) both treat semen analysis as part of the *initial* couple workup, not a later step.
- `<Callout type="clinical-tip">` "Patients who come to me at month 9 sometimes ask if it's too early. The answer is, if you're already booking fertility tests for one partner, book them for both."
- `<Cite n="1" />` NICE, `<Cite n="2" />` ASRM.

## Reasons to Test Earlier Than the 12-Month Rule ← targets: "male factor infertility"
- Known risk factors: prior cancer treatment (chemo/radiation), undescended testes in childhood, varicocele, mumps after puberty, prior chlamydia or other STIs, anabolic steroid or testosterone use, recreational drug use, surgeries on the reproductive tract.
- Female partner age 35+ (compresses the timeline for the whole couple).
- Two or more pregnancy losses without explanation.
- Long-tail prose: "When patients ask me 'when should my husband get a sperm test for TTC?', I tell them: the moment any of these apply, the answer is now, not in six months."

## What a Sperm Analysis Actually Measures ← targets: "sperm count and motility"
- Volume, sperm concentration (count), total motility, progressive motility, morphology (shape), pH, vitality.
- Reference values come from the WHO 2021 sixth edition manual (concentration ≥ 16 million/mL, total motility ≥ 42%, morphology ≥ 4% normal forms).
- Translate the jargon: "motility means how well they swim; morphology means how many are shaped to do the job."
- `<Cite n="3" />` WHO 6th edition.
- Note: a borderline result is not a diagnosis. Sperm production cycles run about 74 days, so abnormal results are usually repeated 8 to 12 weeks later.

## How the Test Is Done (NHS vs US Pathway) ← targets: long-tail "how is a sperm analysis done UK and US"
- UK / NHS pathway: GP refers to the local NHS fertility lab; sample produced on-site or delivered within an hour; usually free at point of care.
- US pathway: ordered by OB-GYN, urologist, or reproductive endocrinologist; in-clinic or accredited lab; out-of-pocket typically $100 to $300; FDA-cleared mail-in and at-home options exist but have known limitations (no morphology, single-snapshot).
- Practical prep: 2 to 5 days of abstinence before sample, no recent fever or illness.

## What Happens After an Abnormal Result ← bridges to related Dr. Rumpa posts
- A single abnormal test is not a verdict. Repeat in 8 to 12 weeks.
- Follow-up may include endocrine blood work (testosterone, FSH, LH, prolactin), urology referral, scrotal ultrasound.
- Lifestyle levers in those 3 months: stop smoking and recreational drugs, cut alcohol, address weight and exercise, review medications (some antidepressants, finasteride, exogenous testosterone), manage heat exposure (saunas, laptops on lap).
